Let's reimagine beauty.

How do young women perceive themselves?

What are the social, financial, and medical costs of poor self-perception?



Now more than ever, girls and young women feel pressured to be beautiful.



Self-esteem is a mitigating factor in how much pressure they feel to be "beautiful".

In a 2017 study, girls with higher self-esteem tended to feel less worried about maintaining their physical appearance than their counterparts with lower self-esteem.




Source: The 2017 Dove Global Girls Beauty and Confidence Report (Diedrichs and Weiner, 2017)

These pressures have been costly for women and society at large, financially, socially, and medically.



The financial and non-financial costs of dissatisfaction with women's bodies has been substantial.



Financial costs can be measured using costs in productivity due to the medical pressures of body dissatisfaction, such as wage losses and reduced employment.
Non-financial costs can be quantified using the years of life lost due to debilitating conditions like depression, or even premature death.


Ultimately, beauty pressures and body dissatisfaction is a pressing public health crisis.




Source: The Real Cost of Beauty Ideals (Dove, 2022)


Source: International Society of Aesthetic Plastic Surgery (ISAPS) International Survey On Aesthetic/Cosmetic Procedures
(ISAPS, 2010; ISAPS, 2013; ISAPS, 2015; ISAPS, 2017; ISAPS, 2019; ISAPS, 2021)


Medically, the impact on women has been two-fold.

Women have been increasingly driven by changing beauty standards and body dissatisfaction to elective cosmetic procedures such as breast augmentations, botox, and liposuction.

Consequently, these non-surgical and surgical procedures can cause unnecessary harm.



Next, women can suffer irreparable harm from low self-esteem and body dissatisfaction.




This can have profound and debilitating impacts on individuals and society, causing mental disorders like eating disorders and even putting women out of work.

What can we do?

1

Promoting media literacy

Increasing media literacy has proven effective in reducing disordered eating and improving "body-related outcomes" such as body satisfaction in boys and reduced "drive for thinness" in both boys and girls
(McLean et al., 2016, p. 19)

2

Reducing social media use

Limiting social media usage by 50% for a few weeks significantly improved adolescents' sentiments about their weight and overall appearance, when compared to peers who continued using social media at high levels (Thai et al., 2023)

3

Cognitive dissonance-based interventions

Actively reflecting on one's experiences with beauty pressures, the costs of pursuing the "thin ideal" and writing about their positive qualities helped to reduce "self-objectification" and negative self-perceptions
(Becker et al., 2013).
